1. Chinese Export Controls

1.1 MOFCOM Decision No. 58 of 2025 (Announced Oct 9, 2025) [1][11]

China's Ministry of Commerce (MOFCOM) and General Administration of Customs announced export controls targeting:

  • Rechargeable lithium-ion batteries (cells and packs) with energy density ≥ 300 Wh/kg at the cell level [1]
  • Battery manufacturing equipment (winding machines, laser die-cutters, electrolyte-injection machines, hot presses, formation/grading cabinets, capacity sorting cabinets) [1]
  • Cathode materials (lithium iron phosphate with specific tap density/capacity, ternary cathode precursors) [1]
  • Artificial graphite anode materials and associated production equipment/technology [1]

These would have required an export license from MOFCOM. [1][12]

1.2 MOFCOM Decision No. 70 of 2025 — SUSPENSION [2][13]

⚠️ IMPORTANT
On November 7, 2025, MOFCOM suspended Decision No. 58. The suspension is effective until November 10, 2026. During this period, the above items can be exported without an export license. [2][13]

Key considerations:

  • The suspension resulted from US-China trade discussions and is explicitly temporary [2]
  • Reinstatement after Nov 10, 2026 is possible and depends on geopolitical relations [2]
  • Companies should plan for the possibility of controls being reinstated [12]
  • General compliance with China's Import and Export Commodity Inspection Law remains mandatory (labeling, packing, shipper registration) [14]

1.4 Decision 58 — Key Questions Investigated

Q1: Does the regulation apply to individual cells, packs, or both?

⚠️ ANSWER: BOTH
Decision 58 explicitly covers "rechargeable lithium-ion batteries — including cells and battery packs" with gravimetric energy density ≥300 Wh/kg (HS code reference: 85076000). There is no exemption for individual cells vs. finished packs. If the cell exceeds 300 Wh/kg, it is controlled regardless of whether it is shipped as a standalone cell or integrated into a pack.

Implication for E4: With a ~350 Wh/kg cell target, both the cells and any packs built from them would fall under the export control if Decision 58 is reinstated. Assembling packs outside China using Chinese cells does not avoid the control — the cells themselves are covered.

Q3: What licensing pathways exist if Decision 58 is reinstated?

If controls are reinstated after Nov 10, 2026, exporting ≥300 Wh/kg cells/packs from China requires an export license from MOFCOM under China's Export Control Law. The process:

  1. Determine if the product is listed — confirm HS code and energy density against the catalogue
  2. Prepare application documentation — product/technical specs, end-use description, consignee details, company credentials
  3. Submit via MOFCOM portal — application reviewed by MOFCOM and General Administration of Customs
  4. Approval timeline — not publicly specified; likely weeks to months; approval is discretionary and may be denied based on end-use or destination country considerations

Practical reality: The licensing pathway exists but is not guaranteed. Approval is at MOFCOM's discretion, and given that the controls were originally enacted as leverage in US-China trade tensions, exports destined for US companies may face heightened scrutiny or denial. This should not be relied upon as a primary mitigation strategy.

1.5 Recommended Strategy: Decoupled Cell Sourcing + Pack Assembly

Rather than sourcing complete battery packs from a single supplier, separating cell sourcing from pack assembly provides maximum flexibility against regulatory, tariff, and supply chain risks.

STRATEGY OVERVIEW
Source cells from multiple qualified suppliers → Ship cells to an independent pack assembler → Assembler builds E4 battery packs to Flyability's design.

Primary path (US market): US cells ≤ 350 Wh/kg (ENPOWER / Amprius) + US pack assembly (Packet Digital / Re:Build). This maximizes US content in the E4 drone system and supports FCC authorization. Cells must be EAR99 (≤ 350 Wh/kg) to avoid US re-export controls on global sales.

Secondary path (non-US markets + US spare batteries): BEI (Korea) cells + EU pack assembly (Leclanché / GRITEC). BEI is the preferred cell source for this configuration because Korean-manufactured cells are not subject to Chinese export controls (Decision 58), US EAR re-export controls, or NDAA entity-of-concern restrictions — making them the only origin with zero regulatory risk at E4 energy densities. Chinese cells (Spard, Grepow, CBAK) remain a viable cost-optimized alternative only while Decision 58 is suspended (currently until Nov 10, 2026).

Why this approach?

Benefit Explanation
Supplier agility If Decision 58 blocks Chinese cell exports, swap to Amprius, ENPOWER, or BEI cells — the assembly line stays the same. No need to re-qualify an entirely new pack supplier.
Tariff optimization If cells are assembled into packs in the US or a third country, the finished pack's country of origin for US customs may change, potentially avoiding the ~28.4% Section 301 tariff on Chinese batteries. Requires HTS classification confirmation.
IP & quality control Flyability owns the pack design, BMS integration, connector design, and quality standards. Consistent output regardless of which cells go in.
Regulatory simplification Decision 58 controls cells AND packs exported from China. But if non-China cells are assembled in the US/EU, neither the cells nor the packs touch China's export controls at all.

2.4 Additional US Gatekeeper: UFLPA Forced-Labor Enforcement [22][23]

⚠️ HIGH-IMPACT IMPORT RISK (often missed)
US Customs (CBP) can detain shipments under UFLPA when goods are made wholly or in part with forced labor from the Xinjiang region or linked entities. This is not a tariff issue; it is a customs release issue. In 2025, DHS/FLETF identified lithium as a high-priority sector for enforcement.
  • Applies at US import regardless of whether the shipment otherwise passes tariff and dangerous-goods requirements
  • Importer must provide traceability evidence to rebut detention (supplier chain, smelter/refinery path, bills of materials, affidavits)
  • Battery shipments with opaque upstream graphite/lithium sourcing have elevated detention risk

2b. US Drone Import Restrictions — FCC Covered List & NDAA [17][18]

🔴 IMPORTANT — US Drone Import Restrictions (Feb 2026)
Beyond export controls and tariffs, the US has enacted import-side restrictions on drones that indirectly affect battery sourcing. The FCC regulates devices that emit radio frequency (RF) energy — drones need FCC equipment authorization to be sold in the US; batteries do not.

Key distinction: The FCC restriction gates the E4 drone's ability to be sold in the US market. The battery is affected only as a component of the drone system. Standalone battery packs (e.g., spare parts) can still be freely imported and sold in the US — the FCC has no jurisdiction over non-RF devices.

Separately, the NDAA Section 842 restricts battery cell origins for federal/DoD procurement only, and targets Chinese entities specifically (not all foreign countries).

2b.1 FCC "Covered List" — UAS Import Ban (Effective Dec 22, 2025) [17]

On December 22, 2025, the FCC added all foreign-produced UAS and UAS critical components to its Covered List, following a national security determination. Equipment on the Covered List is prohibited from receiving FCC equipment authorization, which means it cannot be imported, marketed, or sold in the United States.

"UAS Critical Components" — Explicit Definition [17]

Per the FCC's official FAQ, "UAS critical components" explicitly includes:

  • Data transmission devices
  • Communications systems
  • Flight controllers
  • Ground control stations and UAS controllers
  • Navigation systems
  • Sensors and Cameras
  • Batteries and Battery Management Systems
  • Motors

Exemptions (Temporary — expire Jan 1, 2027) [18]

On January 7, 2026, the FCC granted two temporary exemptions based on a DoW determination:

Exemption Path Requirement Expires
Blue UAS Cleared List UAS and components listed on DCMA's Blue UAS Cleared List (DoD security-vetted) Jan 1, 2027
Buy American Standard UAS and components qualifying as "domestic end products" under 48 CFR 25.101(a) — requires ≥65% US-manufactured content by cost Jan 1, 2027
Conditional Approval Individual exemption request to DoW/DHS via drones@fcc.gov — evaluated case-by-case Case-by-case

How Does the FCC Covered List Actually Affect Batteries? [17]

⚠️ DIRECT vs. INDIRECT IMPACT ON BATTERIES
Direct impact on battery: NONE. Batteries are not RF devices and do not require FCC equipment authorization. The FCC has no mechanism to block a battery at the border. A battery pack — including E4 spare battery packs — can be freely imported, marketed, and sold in the US regardless of country of origin.

The FCC FAQ confirms this: "Generic batteries and similar components are unlikely to have required FCC equipment authorization before, and therefore would not newly require FCC authorization."

Indirect impact: YES — through the drone's authorization. When the E4 drone seeks FCC equipment authorization (which it must, as an RF-emitting device), all critical components — including the battery and BMS — are evaluated as part of the whole system. If Flyability pursues a Conditional Approval, the FCC FAQ states that an "onshoring plan for the manufacturing of all UAS critical components, including components that do not require FCC authorization" is required.

Practical implication: Battery sourcing decisions matter for FCC compliance only insofar as they affect the E4 drone's overall authorization pathway. The battery itself is never directly blocked.

What Counts as a "UAS Critical Component"?

ℹ️ SUB-COMPONENTS vs. ASSEMBLED SUBSYSTEMS
The FCC Covered List targets assembled subsystems, not generic sub-components. Specifically:

Covered as "UAS critical components":
  • Battery pack (the assembled unit with cells, BMS, enclosure, and connectors)
  • Battery Management System (BMS) — the assembled circuit board with firmware
  • Flight controller, ESC, radio transceiver, camera/gimbal, GPS receiver
NOT individually covered:
  • Bare PCBs (unpopulated circuit boards)
  • Generic microcontrollers (MCUs), resistors, capacitors, and other discrete components
  • Individual battery cells (for FCC purposes — though NDAA targets these separately)
  • Wire harnesses, connectors, screws, and mechanical fasteners
Key principle: FCC looks at functional subsystems, not every individual component on a bill of materials. A BMS board assembled in the US from Chinese MCUs would likely still be considered US-produced for FCC purposes. However, a complete BMS unit assembled in China would be classified as foreign-produced.

2c. US-Origin Export Controls (Exports from US to Other Countries)

For batteries exported from the US, there is no blanket export ban. However, the US suppliers' claim that "we cannot export batteries from the US" likely stems from the specific energy density of the cells they manufacture, which triggers strict dual-use export controls.

2c.1 The 350 Wh/kg Threshold (ECCN 3A001.e.1.b) [27]

🔴 CRITICAL E4 PROJECT RISK
Under the US Export Administration Regulations (EAR), secondary (rechargeable) cells with an energy density exceeding 350 Wh/kg are classified as ECCN 3A001.e.1.b. This is a highly controlled "dual-use" item (National Security NS2, Anti-Terrorism AT1).

Because the E4 battery target is ~350 Wh/kg, advanced US suppliers (like Amprius or ENPOWER) producing cells at or above this threshold are subject to strict export licensing requirements. This is almost certainly why they claim they "cannot export" — they mean they cannot export freely without BIS licenses.

How EAR Classification Affects US Battery Exports:

  • Below 350 Wh/kg: Generally classified as EAR99. These can be exported to most countries (including Switzerland, EU, etc.) without a license (NLR - No License Required), except to sanctioned countries (Iran, Russia, etc.) or denied parties.
  • Above 350 Wh/kg (ECCN 3A001.e.1.b): Requires a formal export license from the US Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS) to ship to many countries. While shipping to Switzerland/EU is generally permitted without a license or under an exception, shipping the final drone containing these batteries from Switzerland to certain other countries might trigger US re-export controls.
  • Military/Space Spec: If a battery is "specially designed" for military or space use, it falls under ITAR (USML) or EAR 600-series, which are heavily restricted globally. (E4 should avoid these).

2c.2 The Re-Export Problem [27]

🔴 Why "US Cells + US Assembly" Does NOT Automatically Solve Global Sales
If Flyability sources cells from the US that exceed 350 Wh/kg (ECCN 3A001), those cells carry US export jurisdiction even after they leave the US. The EAR applies extraterritorially: if you integrate a >350 Wh/kg US cell into the E4 drone pack in Switzerland, and then ship that drone to a customer in the Middle East, Asia, or South America, Flyability may need prior authorization from the US government (BIS) before completing the sale.

This is called the "re-export" rule (EAR Part 734). Items subject to the EAR that are exported from the US and then re-exported from a third country remain under US jurisdiction. The threshold for triggering re-export controls depends on the ECCN, the destination country, and whether the item is incorporated into a foreign-made product (the "de minimis" rule — typically 25% US-origin controlled content for most countries, 10% for embargoed countries).

2b.2 NDAA Section 842 — Cell Origin Clause for Federal Procurement [19]

The FY2026 NDAA (Section 842) prohibits the Department of Defense from procuring advanced batteries whose cells are sourced from "foreign entities of concern" (including Chinese companies). This goes beyond the FCC rules by targeting the cell level, not just the finished product.

Q: Can we bypass NDAA by taking Chinese cells and assembling the pack in the US/EU?

A: No. The NDAA strictly closes the "assembly loophole" through its definition of origin.

According to the legislative text (e.g., H.R. 8631 / NDAA FY24 restrictions on companies like CATL, BYD, and EVE Energy), a battery is legally considered "produced by" a banned entity if that entity either:

  1. Assembles or manufactures the final product that uses such battery; OR
  2. Creates or otherwise provides a majority of the components used in such battery.

Because the bare battery cells constitute the "majority of the components" (by function, volume, and value) of a battery pack, buying cells from a Chinese manufacturer means they have "provided a majority of the components." Therefore, the US government considers the entire battery pack to be Chinese-produced, permanently disqualifying it from federal/DoD procurement, regardless of where the BMS was made or where the final pack was assembled.

2b.5 Impact on Chargers and Ground Equipment

When considering the full drone ecosystem, it is important to understand how regulations apply to the battery chargers and ground control stations.

Q: Are battery chargers restricted by FCC or NDAA rules? Do they need to be US-made?

A: Chargers are not explicitly banned by the FCC Covered List, but they are strictly regulated for federal sales under the Trade Agreements Act (TAA) and NDAA. A WTO country is perfectly fine.

  • FCC Covered List (Commercial US Sales): The FCC's December 2025 decision banned "foreign-produced UAS and UAS critical components." However, the official FCC FAQ defines critical components as flight controllers, ground control stations, batteries, and battery management systems (BMS). It does not explicitly list battery chargers. Therefore, chargers are likely exempt from the categorical FCC ban and only require standard FCC Part 15 (unintentional radiator) compliance.
  • Trade Agreements Act (TAA) (Federal/DoD Sales): If Flyability sells the drone system to the US Government (e.g., via GSA schedule), the entire system—including the charger—must be TAA compliant. TAA requires products to be manufactured in the US or a "designated country." WTO GPA countries (like Switzerland, EU nations, Taiwan, South Korea, Japan) are TAA designated countries. China is not. Therefore, a charger produced in Europe or Taiwan is 100% compliant for US federal sales.
  • NDAA Section 848 (Federal/DoD Sales): NDAA Section 848 (FY20) prohibits the DoD from procuring UAS that use "critical components" or a "ground control system" from a covered foreign country (China, Russia, Iran, North Korea). A WTO country is not a covered foreign country.

Strategic takeaway for Chargers: You do not need to manufacture the charger in the US to be compliant. Sourcing the charger from a WTO country (like the EU, Taiwan, or Korea) is a safe, fully compliant strategy for both commercial (FCC) and federal (TAA/NDAA) US markets. Sourcing from China, however, will block federal sales.
