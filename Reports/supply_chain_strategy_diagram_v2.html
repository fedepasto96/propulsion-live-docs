E4 Battery — Phased Supply Chain Strategy

Commercial-first approach: NDAA deprioritized. EVE / Enpower (China) as baseline, Amprius (US-headquartered, China-sourced cells) as exploratory. • Feb 2026

BASELINE PATH EVE / Enpower Cells (China) — Selected for "Works Like" (WL)

High responsiveness, proven facilities, and cost-effective. MOFCOM Decision 58 risk is deemed manageable. Crucially: Cells must be shipped to a US Assembler to ensure the final battery pack contributes enough US-manufactured content to help the E4 drone pass the FCC "Buy American" 65% threshold.

EXPLORATORY PATH Amprius Cells (US) — Evaluated for "Looks Like" (LL)

Highest performance cells. NDAA compliance was initially assumed based on Amprius being a US-headquartered company. However, Amprius sources most of their cells (including the ones relevant to E4) from China, invalidating the NDAA advantage. Additionally, cells are very expensive (~45 CHF/cell), supplier responsiveness is slow, and energy density >350 Wh/kg triggers strict US EAR re-export controls for global sales from Switzerland.

Phased Execution Plan

Phase 1: Works Like (WL) 2026

Execute the initial prototype phase using the validated, cost-effective baseline.

  • Procure EVE / Enpower cells for the 10-15 WL batteries.
  • Establish US assembly partnership (Packet Digital/Re:Build) to ensure FCC pathway is viable.
  • Validate BMS and system performance with EVE / Enpower chemistry.

Phase 2: Looks Like (LL) & Beyond 2027+

Evaluate if Amprius justifies its premium and regulatory overhead.

  • Procure Amprius samples in parallel for testing.
  • Assess EAR re-export burden for global sales.
  • Decision Gate: If Amprius proves superior and viable, switch for LL (250 units) and ML (2000 units). If not, proceed with EVE / Enpower.

⚡ The "No-NDAA" Trade-off

By accepting that the E4 drone will not be sold to the US Department of Defense or federal agencies (ignoring NDAA), Flyability unlocks the ability to use highly responsive, cost-effective Chinese cells (EVE / Enpower). The remaining regulatory hurdle for the US commercial market (FCC) is solved purely through US-based pack assembly, which adds enough domestic value to satisfy the 65% Buy American requirement.
